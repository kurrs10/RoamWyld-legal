The Schengen 90/180 Day Rule

How it works, how to calculate your days, and how to make sure you never overstay.

What Is the Schengen 90/180 Day Rule?

The Schengen Area is a zone of 27 European countries where passport checks at internal borders have been abolished. If you hold a non-EU passport (US, UK, Australian, Canadian, and most others), you are allowed to stay in the Schengen Area for a maximum of 90 days in any rolling 180-day period.

This is not 90 days per calendar year, per trip, or per entry. It is 90 days within any 180-day window — and that window rolls forward every day. This is what makes the rule confusing and what catches travelers off guard.

Important: Overstaying the Schengen limit can result in a fine, immediate deportation, and a ban from re-entering the Schengen Area for up to 5 years. Border officers check entry and exit stamps when you leave.

Which Countries Are in the Schengen Area?

As of 2026, the Schengen Area includes 27 countries:

  • Original members: Germany, France, Spain, Italy, Portugal, Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg, Austria, Greece
  • Nordic countries: Sweden, Denmark, Finland, Norway, Iceland
  • Eastern Europe: Poland, Czech Republic, Slovakia, Hungary, Slovenia, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania
  • Other: Switzerland, Liechtenstein, Malta, Croatia, Romania, Bulgaria

Note: The UK, Ireland, Cyprus, and non-EU countries like Albania, North Macedonia, and Serbia are not in the Schengen Area, even if they are geographically in Europe. Time spent in those countries does not count toward your 90-day limit.

How to Calculate Your Schengen Days

The 90/180 rule uses a rolling window, not fixed dates. To know how many days you have remaining on any given date, you must look back 180 days from that date and count every day you spent in the Schengen Area during that window.

Example: You want to know how many Schengen days you have on June 1, 2026. Count back 180 days to December 3, 2025. Add up every day you were physically inside a Schengen country between December 3, 2025 and June 1, 2026. Subtract that number from 90. The result is your remaining days.

Both your entry day and exit day count as full Schengen days. If you fly in on Monday and out on Friday, that is 5 days used — not 4.

What Is the EU Entry/Exit System (EES)?

The EU's Entry/Exit System (EES) went live in April 2026. Instead of relying on passport stamps — which are easy to miss, smudge, or dispute — EU border crossings now record your entry and exit digitally. This means EU border officers will have an automatic, precise record of every day you have spent in the Schengen Area.

EES makes accidental overstays much harder to talk your way out of. The digital record is definitive. Tracking your own Schengen days accurately has never been more important.

Common Schengen Mistakes

  • Thinking it resets every 6 months. The window is rolling, not fixed. Spending 90 days in January–March does not mean you can re-enter in July — it depends on when those 90 days fall relative to today's 180-day lookback window.
  • Not counting transit days. If you land in a Schengen airport and pass through immigration — even briefly — that day counts.
  • Assuming non-Schengen EU countries are included. Time in Ireland or Cyprus does not count toward or against your limit.
  • Relying on passport stamps. With EES now live, the digital record is what matters, not the stamp.
  • Calculating from your last entry date. The window looks back from today, not from when you entered.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the 90-day limit apply to each Schengen country separately?

No. The limit applies to the entire Schengen Area combined. A week in France, two weeks in Germany, and a week in Italy all count toward the same 90-day total.

I have a US passport. Do I need a visa for the Schengen Area?

US citizens can enter the Schengen Area without a visa for stays up to 90 days in any 180-day period. Once ETIAS launches (expected late 2026), you will need to pre-register online for €7 before each trip — but this is not a visa, just a travel authorization similar to the US ESTA.

Does time spent in the UK, Ireland, or other non-Schengen European countries count?

No. Only time physically inside a Schengen member state counts toward your 90-day limit. The UK, Ireland, Cyprus, and non-EU Balkan countries are separate.

What is ETIAS and how does it affect my travel?

ETIAS (European Travel Information and Authorisation System) is a pre-travel authorization expected to launch in late 2026. It will apply to travelers from 59 visa-exempt nationalities, including the US, UK, and Australia. It costs €7, is valid for 3 years, and must be obtained before you board. It does not change the 90/180 day limit — it is an additional step, like the US ESTA.

I overstayed my Schengen limit. What happens?

When you exit the Schengen Area, border officers check your entry and exit records. If you overstayed, consequences range from a warning and fine (for short overstays) to a multi-year entry ban. With the EU's new EES digital border system now live, overstays are automatically flagged. If you believe you may have overstayed, consult a lawyer or your country's embassy before attempting to exit.
